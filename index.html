At Bruin Electric Autonomous Racing, our goal is to enable researchers tackle difficult problems concerning high-speed autonomy through scalable methods. To achieve that, we've made our miniature platforms open-source as to encourage autonomous races to race together and have fun!

BEAR x IEEE

In an effort to realize our goal of making autonomous racing fun, we've connected with the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers at the University of California, Los Angeles to allow undergraduate students to race their autonomous vehicles against each other. Check out what the BEAR team has to say:

"BEAR is a mini F1 sandbox for roboticists. It’s racing, with code.”
Jesse Cha – UCLA PhD Candidate / BEAR Research Lead

"The BEAR curriculum is full of surprises. You not only have the opportunity to engineer your own customizable RC car, but enjoy the experience of racing your design against others. It's really self-rewarding on its own."
Jeremy Louie – Curriculum Developer / BEAR Undergraduate Researcher

"BEAR provides an opportunity for interested students of all disciplines to gain experience in both hardware and software engineering."
Aaron Kuo – Curriculum Developer / IEEE Aircopter Lead

Our Projects

Outside of delivering accessible curriculum to undergraduate students, we also perform research that primarily focuses on fully autonomous driving radio control cars. Feel free to check out our breakthroughs.

The Science Behind MACHInE

Moving at 24 mph (350 mph to scale) these Multi-Agent Autonomous Cars for High-Speed, Indoor Environments (MACHInE) dominate the highway scenario without the need for any driver.

Collision Avoidance with Overtaking Maneuvers

The Research Behind OP-CAS

With an end-to-end autonomous driving algorithm, the Overtaking Procedure for Collision Avoidance Systems (OP-CAS) is not only cheap on hardware, but also easy to deploy on embedded systems.
